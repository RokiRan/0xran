A castle is a structure you cannot easily leave. The walls are thick because the cost of building them is sunk; the rooms are organized around a single owner; the people inside are bound by the architecture.

A camp is the opposite. It is small enough to set up in an afternoon, exposed enough that you can see the weather coming, and organized around the people in it rather than the people who might arrive. If the camp stops being useful, you fold the tent and walk away.

## Why this site is a camp

Everything you read here is hosted as static files. There is no database, no backend, no user accounts, no telemetry. The dependencies fit on one screen. If I disappear tomorrow, the site still loads.

I used to build software the other way: rich platforms with steep moats and a long-term roadmap. It worked, technically. But every project I shipped as a castle became something I dreaded maintaining, and eventually something I abandoned entirely — which was worse than not having built it at all.

> The best projects I have shipped are the ones I could leave on Friday and not feel a Sunday-morning dread about.

## Three rules I keep

- Ship the smallest thing that works, then leave it alone for six months.
- If I cannot describe the whole project on one page, it is too big.
- If I am the only one who can fix it, it is too coupled.

These rules are not universal advice. They are the rules I need, given how I work. You probably need different ones, and the point is not the rules themselves — it is knowing what shape your work takes when no one is watching.

For me, that shape is a camp.
